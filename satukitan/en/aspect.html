Tense and Aspect

Concepts of Tense and Aspect

Tense and Aspect are both concepts that determine the span of time a word refers to. Tense points to a reference point on the timeline, while aspect describes how an action unfolds around that reference point.

Examples of tense and aspect (English)
Aspect \ Tense Past tense Present tense
Perfective aspect He ate a mandarin. He eats a mandarin.
Continuous aspect He was eating a mandarin. He is eating a mandarin.

The past-tense perfective example "ate" uses the past as its reference point (past tense) and refers to the whole action of "eat" around that point (perfective). For example, when you say "I start work at six." the context might mean "I usually start work at six." or "Today, I start work at six." In Satu Kitan, tense and aspect are specified independently and precisely, so meanings do not change with context. However, finer shades of meaning that are not strictly defined may still vary with context.

How Tense and Aspect are Expressed

Tense

Satu Kitan has three tenses: past tense, present tense, and future tense, which are marked by suffixes. When both a tense suffix and an aspect suffix are attached, the tense suffix usually comes after the aspect suffix.

Suffix Tense Intuitive meaning
none Present tense do
ta (ta) Past tense did
ka (ka) Future tense will do

Aspect

Satu Kitan recognizes six aspects: perfective aspect, perfect aspect, continuous aspect, progressive aspect, completive aspect, and inchoative aspect. Aspect assignment is more complex than tense. If a word carries an aspect-marking suffix, that suffix determines the aspect; if no suffix is present, the aspect follows from the word's meaning type. Some grammatical words define aspect directly, while other function words, nominals, or auxiliaries typically do not affect aspect.

Suffix Process words Result words State words Intuitive meaning
none Perfective Perfect Continuous do; remain in a state
so (so) Progressive Progressive Progressive be doing
ti (ti) Completive Completive Completive finish doing
ki (ki) Inchoative Inchoative Inchoative begin doing

Some morphemes change a word's meaning type when used as suffixes, and this in turn affects aspect. For example, attaching the state suffix "ni (ni) / always ..." to a base produces a state word regardless of the original type.

Semantic Definitions of Tense and Aspect

In Satu Kitan, tense and aspect together precisely determine the temporal interval a word refers to. Process, result, and state words typically mention events or states with a temporal extent. In this section we use the following symbols to define tense and aspect formally.

  • The current time in context S
  • The temporal interval which the event denoted by the word occurs E = [ E start , E end ]
  • The temporal interval the word actually mentions M = [ M start , M end ]

Tense

Tense indicates where the interval a word mentions is located on the timeline.

Past Tenss (ta suffixed)
M end < S

The interval the word mentions lies entirely in the past.
Example: I ate a mandarin yesterday.

Present Tense (not suffixed)
M start ≦ S ≦ M end

The interval the word mentions spans across the present into past and future. This is typically used for events that end now or in the near future, or for states/properties that persist indefinitely.
Example: I am studying mathematics.

Future Tense (ka suffixed)
S < M start

The interval the word mentions lies entirely in the future.
Example: I will go to Kyoto tomorrow.

Aspect

Aspect characterizes the relationship between the interval a word mentions and the entire event it mentions.

Perfective Aspect (process words, not suffixed)
M = E

The word mentions the whole completed event.
Example: I walked from home to school (referring to the whole trip from departure to arrival).

Perfect Aspect (result words, not suffixed)
M = E = [ E start , + ∞ ]

The word mentions an event that began at some moment and (unless otherwise specified) continues indefinitely into the future. Result words commonly pair with process words, so this aspect can be thought of as referring to the state after an event has finished.
Example: The window has broken (there was a breaking event in the past and the broken state continues).

Continuous Aspect (state words, not suffixed)
M = E = [ - ∞ , + ∞ ]

The word mentions a universal event or property that (unless otherwise specified) extends from the past into the future.
Example: Apples are generally red (referring to a time-independent property).

Progressive Aspect (so suffixed)
E start < M start , M end < E end

The word mentions a short subinterval cut out from the whole event. It does not specify the temporal extent of the entire event.
Example: He is running now (referring only to the present moment).

Completive Aspect (ti suffixed)
E start < M start , M end = E end

The word mentions a short subinterval immediately before the event's end. It does not specify the full temporal extent of the event.
Example: He finished cooking.

Inchoative Aspect (ki suffixed)
E start = M start , M end < E end

The word mentions a short subinterval immediately after the event begins. It does not specify the whole temporal extent of the event.
Example: It has just started raining.

Nuances of Tense and Aspect Combinations

When the continuous aspect (which expresses a general property) is combined with past tense, it often implicitly mentions the end of that property in the present. This can carry the nuance "it used to be the case, but not anymore", although this is not guaranteed. The perfect aspect combined with past tense can similarly imply a change from the past to the present. However, these are pragmatic nuances, not strict semantic definitions, so avoid relying on them in formal arguments. To indicate the end of an event explicitly, use complement words mentioning times.

Aspect-related Suffixes

ni (ni) / always ... (habitual)

In Satu Kitan the habitual sense is marked with the suffix ni. Habitual meaning can be treated as a distinct "habitual aspect", but in Satu Kitan it is handled as the continuous aspect representing an enduring property of doing something.

Similarly, the following suffixes can turn verbs into state words.

  • ge (ge) / can ...
  • pi (pi) / should ...
  • pipi (pipi) / must ...
  • poni (poni) / please ...

Complement Words mentioning Times

Using complement words that mention times makes explicit the interval M that the verb mentions. These complement words take precedence over aspects that would otherwise imply an unbounded continuation, allowing expressions such as "This house will be red until tomorrow" where the continuous aspect is used together with a bounded time.

nav(nan) / at

Modify a time-denoting noun to form an adjunct that indicates a time position that is included in the interval M the word refers to.

tev(ten) / to

Modify a time-denoting noun to form an adjunct that indicates the time position at which the interval M ends. Note that tev can also mark conceptual or spatial endpoints; when you need to make it clear you mean a time endpoint, use nastev (nasten).

kev(ken) / from

Modify a time-denoting noun to form an adjunct that indicates the time position at which the interval M begins. Note that kev can also mark conceptual or spatial starting points; when you need to make it clear you mean a time starting point, use naskev (nasken).
