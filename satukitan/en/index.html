Introduction

satu kitav (pronounced: Satu Kitan) is a logical and highly precise constructed language. It is a neutral language not belonging to any specific country (in its setting), developed to allow everyone to communicate and discuss equally, with no ambiguity and strict precision.

These characters are called ritu kitav (Ritu Kitan). If Ritu Kitan cannot be used, you may substitute the Latin letters shown below each character.
Blue letters are used only for loanwords and are not used for native Satu Kitan words.
Red letters are special characters with both grammatical and phonetic meaning, called symbol-like characters.

In this page, Satu Kitan pronunciation is shown using lowercase. In general, reading as written in lowercase will give the correct Satu Kitan pronunciation, but for sounds not found in English or those with large differences, UPPERCASE is used as an exception.

Note that words are pronounced exactly as they are written (also lowercase). Each letter (or letter combination) always has the same sound, so you can read it straight from the spelling without silent letters or irregular rules.

Basic Grammar

Words and Parts of Speech

Satu Kitan has four parts of speech: nouns, verbs, modifiers, and conjunctions. These are distinguished by adding a specific consonant at the end of the word. For example, "giputu (giputu) / be understood" can be inflected into all four parts of speech.

Conjunction form

The inflection patterns are the same for all words. That is, all words ending with s are verbs, all words ending with f are conjunctions, and all words ending with a vowel + v are modifiers.

Verb Sentences

Here is an example of a grammatically correct sentence in Satu Kitan. A typical Satu Kitan sentence (verb sentence) starts with a verb as the predicate, followed by the object, and ends with ".".

Accent

Place the accent on the last syllable of the verb. In the above sentence, "tu" is pronounced higher and longer. Other words are not accented.

Sentence Structure

In the above sentence, "hanyu fov / that problem" is the object of the verb "giputus / is understood". Grammatically, verb sentences have no subject.
Also, modifiers modify nouns or verbs from behind. In the above, the modifier "fov / that" modifies the noun "hanyu / problem" before it.

Note that "'" and "," are pronounced characters.

Compound Words

In Satu Kitan, words can be compounded by inserting s between noun forms. By compounding words, additional information or attributes are added to the leftmost word.
Compounding can be done regardless of part of speech.

Rules of Sentence Structure

The grammar of Satu Kitan can be formally defined as follows.
Here, "A B" means that B follows A.

  • Noun-element is one of the following:
    • Noun
    • Verb-sentence ;
    • Noun-element Modifier-element
  • Modifier-element is one of the following:
    • Modifier
    • ' Verb-sentence ,
    • ' Noun-element ,
  • Verb-element is one of the following:
    • Verb
    • Verb-element Modifier-element
  • Verb-sentence is one of the following:
    • Verb-element
    • Verb-sentence Noun-element
  • Sentence is one of the following:
    • Noun-element
    • Verb-sentence
    • Sentence Conjunction Noun-element
    • Sentence Conjunction Verb-sentence

There is no ambiguity in these rules. That is, every valid Satu Kitan sentence can be interpreted according to these rules, and there is always only one interpretation. As a result, Satu Kitan does not have semantic ambiguities such as "which word a modifier modifies" or "which is the object of a verb".

Word Categories

Satu Kitan words are classified into five types according to the kind of meaning they have (regardless of quality): process words, result words, function words, name words, and complementizers.

Process Words

Words that conjugate as verbs and whose final syllable consonant is k (k). They conjugate for all parts of speech.
They express actions involving change or processes of change.
Basically, process words and result words exist as pairs that differ only in the final consonant of the last syllable (e.g., kika / be proved and kita / correct).

Result Words

Words that conjugate as verbs and whose final syllable consonant is t (t). They conjugate for all parts of speech.
They express states after some change has occurred, or properties added as a result.

Function Words

Words that conjugate as verbs but are neither process words nor result words. They conjugate for all parts of speech.
Momentary actions, continuous actions, and states that cannot be defined as having a beginning or end are classified as function words.

Name Words

Words that represent names of things or concepts. They conjugate as nouns and modifiers.
As modifiers, they have almost the same meaning as the English “A’s”, “of A”, and broadly express belonging, possession, cause, etc. of the modified target.

Complement Words

Used basically only as modifiers, and form complementary objects.

Process/Result Words and Tense

In Satu Kitan, words that are unified in other languages may be distinguished as process words and result words.
Let’s look at the difference using the example of "gikus / be known (action)" and "gitus / be known (state)".

Tense Process Result
Present gikus / be known
The event of "being known" is occurring. Rarely used. 		gitus / be known
The state of "known" is held.
Progressive gikussos / be known
The event of "being known" is happening right now. 		gitussos / be known
The state of "known" is being held right now. Rarely used.
Past gikustas / was known
The event of "being known" occurred at some point in the past. 		gitustas / was known
Was known at some point in the past. Implies it is not known now.
Future gikuskas / will be known
The event of "being known" will occur at some point in the future. 		gituskas / will be known
At some point in the future, it will be in a known state.
